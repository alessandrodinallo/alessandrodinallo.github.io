2025

  1. DemRes
    Juno (2007), dir. Jason Reitman
    The partnership context of first parenthood
    Alessandro Di Nallo
    Demographic Research, 2025
    When, and in which partnership, do people in the UK have their first child? Following individuals from their teens into adulthood, I show that those from working-class families become parents earlier and more often outside marriage, most often within cohabitation, while those from higher-class families delay parenthood and tend to have their first child within marriage. Cohabiting first births have become more common across all groups over successive cohorts, yet the class gradient in the timing and context of first parenthood persists.
    DOI
  2. Demography
    The Way We Were (1973), dir. Sydney Pollack
    Sleeping with the enemy. Partners’ heterogamy by political preferences and union dissolution
    Bruno Arpino and Alessandro Di Nallo
    Demography, 2025
    Do partners with opposing political views stay together? Drawing on nearly 29,000 British couples between 1991 and 2019, we find that political heterogamy is associated with a higher risk of union dissolution, an association that grew more visible around the Brexit referendum. The pattern suggests that political differences can erode relationship stability much like other forms of value mismatch, and that a polarised climate can make those differences harder to bridge.
    DOI
  3. JFEI
    Pieces of a Woman (2020), dir. Kornél Mundruczó
    How perceived job insecurity, voluntary unemployment and involuntary job loss shape couples’ life satisfaction
    Florence Lébert, Oliver Lipps, and Alessandro Di Nallo
    Journal of Family and Economic Issues, 2025
    Employment shocks are not all alike. Using German panel data, we compare how perceived job insecurity, voluntary unemployment and involuntary job loss each shape the life satisfaction of both partners, and find that the sharpest declines follow involuntary job loss. Both partners’ well-being responds, not only that of the person who loses the job, underscoring that employment shocks are experienced at the level of the couple rather than the individual.
    DOI

  5. EJPH
    Raining Stones (1993), dir. Ken Loach
    Women’s physical health around live births and pregnancy losses
    Alessandro Di Nallo
    European Journal of Public Health, 2025
    How does the body fare around a pregnancy? Tracking women’s physical health over time, I find a decline around births that is more pronounced, and slower to recover, among women who experienced a miscarriage or a stillbirth. The results point to pregnancy loss as an under-recognised marker of women’s longer-term physical health, not only of their emotional well-being.

  2. JMF
    Two Days, One Night (2014), dir. Jean-Pierre & Luc Dardenne
    How much his or her job loss influences fertility. A couple approach
    Alessandro Di Nallo and Oliver Lipps
    Journal of Marriage and Family, 2023
    Does losing a job change a couple’s plans to have children? Using UK and German longitudinal data, we find that unions hit by job loss, and especially by the woman’s, are markedly less likely to go on to have a child. The effect concentrates in the years right after the job loss, consistent with couples postponing childbearing when economic security is threatened.
    DOI
  3. EJP
    Kramer vs. Kramer (1979), dir. Robert Benton
    The intergenerational transmission of family dissolution
    Alessandro Di Nallo and Daniel Oesch
    European Journal of Population, 2023
    Is divorce inherited? Using British cohort data, we show that parental union dissolution raises the chance that children’s own partnerships dissolve, and that this intergenerational transmission is conditioned by social-class origin. Children from more advantaged backgrounds are partly buffered, so the reproduction of family instability is stratified rather than uniform.
    DOI
  4. Pop. Studies
    The Florida Project (2017), dir. Sean Baker
    Repartnering of women in the USA: The interplay between motherhood and socioeconomic status
    Alessandro Di Nallo, Katya Ivanova, and Nicoletta Balbo
    Population Studies, 2023
    After a break-up, who finds a new partner? Analysing US women, we find that motherhood substantially lowers the chances of repartnering for the low-educated, while it matters far less for the highly educated. For less-educated mothers, the combined demands of single parenthood and scarcer economic resources appear to narrow the opportunities to form a new union.
    DOI
